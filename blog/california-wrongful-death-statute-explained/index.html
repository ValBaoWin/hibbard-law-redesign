California's Wrongful Death Statute: What Families Need to Know

Who can file, what damages are recoverable, and why the two-year deadline is critical.

N
Noel Hibbard, Esq.
Managing Attorney

Losing a loved one due to someone else's negligence is one of the most devastating experiences a family can face. California law provides a path to civil justice through the wrongful death statute — but there are important legal requirements, deadlines, and limitations that families must understand.

What Is a Wrongful Death Claim?

A wrongful death claim is a civil lawsuit filed by the surviving family members of a person who died due to another party's negligent, reckless, or intentional conduct. It is separate from any criminal prosecution — a party can face both criminal charges and civil liability for the same act.

Common causes of wrongful death claims in California include car accidents, truck accidents, workplace accidents, medical malpractice, DUI collisions, and defective products.

Who Can File a Wrongful Death Lawsuit in California?

Under California Code of Civil Procedure § 377.60, the following individuals may bring a wrongful death claim:

  • The deceased person's surviving spouse or domestic partner
  • Surviving children
  • Grandchildren (if the deceased's children are also deceased)
  • Any other person who was financially dependent on the deceased, including putative spouses, stepchildren, and parents

The Two-Year Deadline — Do Not Wait

California law imposes a two-year statute of limitations on wrongful death claims. This means you must file your lawsuit within two years of the date of death — or you lose your right to pursue compensation entirely, with very limited exceptions.

Two years may feel like a long time, but building a wrongful death case requires extensive investigation: gathering evidence, interviewing witnesses, retaining expert witnesses, obtaining medical records, and reconstructing the circumstances of the death. This work takes time, and evidence fades. The sooner you contact an attorney, the stronger your case.

"Families grieving a loss deserve time to heal — but the legal clock doesn't pause. Contacting an attorney early protects your rights while giving you the space to focus on what matters."

What Damages Can Be Recovered?

Surviving family members may be entitled to compensation for:

  • Loss of financial support the deceased would have provided
  • Loss of household services (childcare, domestic contributions)
  • Funeral and burial expenses
  • Loss of love, companionship, comfort, and moral support
  • Loss of training and guidance (particularly relevant for minor children)

Note that California does not allow wrongful death plaintiffs to recover for their own grief or emotional distress — those damages are addressed in a separate "survival action" claim, which may be filed alongside the wrongful death suit.
