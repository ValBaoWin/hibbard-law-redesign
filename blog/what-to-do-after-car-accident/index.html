What to Do Immediately After a Car Accident in California

The decisions you make in the hours and days after a car accident can significantly affect your legal rights and recovery.

N
Noel Hibbard, Esq.
Managing Attorney

Being involved in a car accident is a stressful, disorienting experience. Even if you believe the accident was minor, the steps you take immediately afterward can have a profound impact on your health, your insurance claim, and your legal rights. Here is a step-by-step guide from our attorneys.

1. Check for Injuries — Call 911

Before anything else, check yourself and your passengers for injuries. Call 911 immediately, even if injuries seem minor. Some injuries — particularly whiplash, concussions, and internal injuries — may not present symptoms for hours or even days. A police report is also essential documentation for any insurance claim or lawsuit.

2. Move to Safety (If Possible)

If the vehicles are drivable and it is safe to do so, move them to the side of the road to prevent further accidents. Turn on your hazard lights. If anyone is seriously injured, do not move them unless there is an immediate danger (such as fire) — wait for emergency responders.

3. Document Everything at the Scene

Use your phone to photograph and video the scene thoroughly:

  • All vehicles involved — damage, positions, license plates
  • Skid marks, road conditions, traffic signs, and signals
  • Your own visible injuries
  • The other driver's license, insurance card, and registration
  • Contact information for all witnesses

4. Do Not Admit Fault

Be cautious about what you say at the scene. Even a casual "I'm sorry" can be interpreted as an admission of fault. Stick to exchanging information and cooperating with law enforcement. Do not speculate about what happened or what caused the accident.

5. Seek Medical Attention Immediately

Even if you feel fine, see a doctor the same day. This creates a medical record that links your injuries to the accident — critical if you later file a claim. Gaps in medical treatment are one of the most common arguments insurers use to minimize or deny claims.

6. Notify Your Insurance Company

Report the accident to your insurance company promptly, but be careful about what you say. Provide only factual information. Do not give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company before consulting an attorney.

7. Contact a Personal Injury Attorney

Before accepting any settlement offer — no matter how reasonable it seems — consult with a personal injury attorney. Insurance companies often make early lowball offers knowing that injured victims don't yet know the full extent of their damages. Once you accept a settlement, you typically cannot seek additional compensation later.

"The insurance company's first offer is almost never their best offer. We negotiate to ensure you receive full compensation — for medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering."
