Understanding Your Workers' Compensation Benefits in California

A complete guide to the benefits available to injured California workers — and how to make sure you receive everything you're entitled to.

N
Noel Hibbard, Esq.
Managing Attorney

If you've been injured at work in California, you may be entitled to workers' compensation benefits — regardless of who was at fault. California's workers' compensation system is designed to provide prompt, fair compensation to injured workers without the need for lengthy litigation. But navigating the system isn't always straightforward.

Here is a comprehensive overview of the four main categories of benefits available under California workers' compensation law, and what you need to know to protect your rights.

1. Medical Care Benefits

California workers' compensation law entitles injured workers to all "reasonably necessary" medical treatment related to their work injury. This includes doctor visits, hospital care, surgery, prescription medications, physical therapy, and other medical services.

Medical treatment is authorized and paid for by your employer's workers' compensation insurance carrier — not by you. However, insurers frequently try to limit or deny treatment through a process called Utilization Review (UR). If your treatment is denied, you have the right to challenge that decision through an Independent Medical Review (IMR).

"The insurance company's goal is to minimize costs. Your goal is full recovery. Those objectives are often in direct conflict — which is why having an attorney matters."

2. Temporary Disability Benefits

If your injury prevents you from working while you recover, you may be entitled to temporary disability (TD) indemnity — payments that replace a portion of the wages you lose during your recovery period.

Temporary disability payments are generally equal to two-thirds of your average weekly wages, up to a statutory maximum. These payments continue until your doctor releases you to return to work, or your condition reaches what's called "maximum medical improvement" (MMI).

3. Permanent Disability Benefits

If your injury results in lasting impairment — after your condition has stabilized and you've reached MMI — you may be entitled to permanent disability (PD) benefits.

The amount of your permanent disability benefit depends on several factors, including the nature and severity of your injury, your age, your occupation, and the date of your injury. Permanent disability is expressed as a percentage from 0% (no disability) to 100% (total disability), and the rating determines the amount and duration of your payments.

4. Supplemental Job Displacement Benefit

If your work-related injury results in a permanent disability and your employer cannot accommodate your work restrictions, you may be entitled to a Supplemental Job Displacement Benefit (SJDB) — a voucher that can be used to fund retraining or skill enhancement at state-approved schools.

What to Do If Your Claim Is Denied

Insurance companies deny valid workers' compensation claims far too often. If your claim has been denied — or if you believe you're not receiving the benefits you deserve — contact an experienced workers' compensation attorney immediately.
